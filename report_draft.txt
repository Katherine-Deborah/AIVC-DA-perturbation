  Section 1: Introduction

  Parkinson's disease (PD) is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder characterized by the loss of
  dopaminergic (DA) neurons in the substantia nigra. Understanding what transcriptional programs govern DA
   neuron identity — and how genetic perturbations shift cells toward or away from that state — is
  critical for developing therapeutic targets. However, systematically testing hundreds of gene knockdowns
   experimentally is prohibitively expensive and slow.

  AI virtual cell (AIVC) models offer a computational alternative: given single-cell RNA-seq data from
  CRISPR perturbation screens, these models learn to predict how any given gene knockdown will reshape the
   transcriptional landscape of a cell. In this work, we benchmark several AIVC models on two human
  iPSC-derived neuron perturbation datasets — GSE152988 and GSE124703 — with the goal of identifying which
   perturbations most strongly drive cells toward a DA neuron transcriptional state. This represents an
  initial step toward a systematic gene ranking pipeline for experimental validation in PD research.

  Section 2: Literature Review

  2.1 scGen (Lopez et al., 2018)
  scGen is a variational autoencoder (VAE) that models perturbation effects via latent space arithmetic.
  It encodes control and perturbed cells into a shared latent space, computes a perturbation "delta
  vector" (the difference between mean latent representations of perturbed vs. control cells), and applies
   this delta to unseen control cells to predict their perturbed state. scGen is conceptually simple and
  fast to train, but is limited to perturbations seen during training — it cannot generalize to novel
  knockdowns because it has no mechanism to reason about gene relationships.

  2.2 GEARS (Roohani et al., 2023)
  GEARS (Graph-Enhanced Perturbation Prediction) addresses scGen's generalization limitation by
  incorporating a gene co-expression graph (derived from the STRING database) as an inductive bias. It
  trains a graph neural network (GNN) where each node is a gene and edges represent co-expression
  relationships. By reasoning over the gene network, GEARS can predict the effect of knocking down a gene
  it has never seen perturbed during training, as long as that gene has known co-expression relationships
  with genes that were seen. GEARS is the current standard baseline for perturbation prediction and is
  particularly well-suited to the unseen perturbation evaluation setting.

  2.3 scFoundation (Hao et al., 2024)
  scFoundation is a large-scale foundation model pretrained on 50 million human single-cell transcriptomes
   using a masked gene expression modeling objective over a fixed 19,264-gene vocabulary. Rather than
  being a perturbation model itself, scFoundation acts as a feature extractor — generating rich,
  context-aware gene embeddings (shape: cells × 19,264 × 512) that can be used as node features in
  downstream models like GEARS. The hypothesis is that these richer embeddings encode biological priors
  (gene function, co-regulation) that improve prediction of unseen perturbations beyond what the STRING
  graph alone provides.

  2.4 CPA (Lotfollahi et al., 2021)
  CPA (Compositional Perturbation Autoencoder) extends the VAE framework by learning disentangled latent
  representations for cell identity, perturbation effect, and covariate (e.g., batch). This
  compositionality allows CPA to predict the effect of perturbation combinations not seen during training
  by adding their individual latent vectors. CPA is particularly relevant for multi-perturbation settings.

  2.5 STATE (2024)
  STATE (Structured Attention for Transcriptional Effect prediction) introduces a transformer-based
  architecture that attends over gene expression profiles to model perturbation effects. By incorporating
  structured attention mechanisms, it captures long-range gene-gene interactions that GNN-based methods
  may miss.

  2.6 scGenePT (Hao et al., 2024)
  scGenePT augments scGen with gene-level text embeddings derived from large language models (LLMs)
  trained on biological literature. Each gene is represented not only by its expression level but also by
  a natural language description of its function. This allows the model to leverage biological knowledge
  encoded in text to improve generalization to unseen perturbations.

  Section 3: Data

  We use the GSE152988 dataset (Tian & Kampmann, 2021) as our primary benchmark. This dataset contains
  iPSC-derived neurons subjected to CRISPRi (transcriptional repression) perturbations targeting 184
  genes, yielding 32,300 cells × 33,538 genes. Raw integer count matrices were normalized to 10,000 counts
   per cell and log1p-transformed. We selected the top 5,000 highly variable genes (HVGs) to reduce memory
   requirements for our GPU (NVIDIA GTX 1650 Ti, 4 GB VRAM).

  The dataset contains 437 unperturbed control cells and 31,863 cells with single-gene knockdowns, with a
  median of ~175 cells per perturbation.

  Section 4: Methods (partial — fill in GEARS results)

  Train/test split. We use two evaluation protocols to assess different aspects of model performance:

  - Seen perturbation evaluation (scGen): 20% of cells from 20 randomly selected perturbations are
  withheld for testing; the model is trained on the remaining cells including 80% of those same
  perturbations. This tests reconstruction quality.
  - Unseen perturbation evaluation (GEARS): 46 entire perturbations are held out from training entirely
  under the "simulation" split. The model must predict their effects without having observed any cells
  from those conditions. This tests true generalization.

  Mean expression baseline. As a sanity check, we implement a trivial baseline that predicts the global
  mean expression of all training perturbed cells, regardless of which gene is knocked down. This baseline
   is important context — because single-gene perturbations affect relatively few genes, overall Pearson
  correlation is high for all methods including this baseline. The more informative metric is Pearson
  correlation on the top-20 differentially expressed genes (DEGs).

  Metrics. For each held-out perturbation we compute: (1) Pearson correlation across all 5,000 genes
  between predicted and true mean expression; (2) Pearson correlation restricted to the top-20 most
  differentially expressed genes (DEGs); (3) mean squared error (MSE); (4) R². All reported values are
  averages across held-out perturbations.
